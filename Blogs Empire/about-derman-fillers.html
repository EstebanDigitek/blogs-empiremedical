According to the American Society of Plastic Surgeons, U.S. patients underwent about 2.6 million dermal filler procedures in 2018, up 2% from the previous year. Among minimally invasive cosmetic procedures, only treatment with medications derived from botulinum toxin type A (such as Botox®) was more popular.

Why are dermal fillers so popular? Patients and providers alike appreciate their relative affordability, which makes them accessible to a broad audience, and their minimally invasive nature, which enables treatment during the course of a handful of short office visits with local anesthetic only.

Dermal fillers have other advantages as well. The most widely used injectable dermal fillers produce durable but temporary results, typically lasting a few months to two years. Hyaluronic acid (HA) filler, by far the most popular type, is easily reversible in most cases. By contrast, certain other face and body augmentation procedures may require surgical intervention to reverse or correct.

Above all else, dermal fillers are popular with patients and providers because they work. Collectively, dermal fillers are FDA approved to treat a variety of facial lines, wrinkles, low-volume areas, and other cosmetic imperfections and signs of aging.

First up, a working definition of “dermal fillers” and a closer look at their approved uses.

What Are Dermal Fillers?

Dermal fillers, or soft tissue fillers, are injectable treatments indicated for the mitigation or correction of a variety of age- and non-age-related cosmetic complaints. They reduce the appearance of cosmetic defects such as wrinkles, lines, scars, divots, and hollow areas by increasing skin volume.

Depending on their type and formulation, dermal fillers may act by temporarily hydrating the treated area, adding or stimulating the production of collagen, or leaving stable microstructures that add volume under the skin. They are most often used on the face but can be applied elsewhere on the body as well.

FDA Approved Uses for Dermal Fillers

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), which regulates pharmaceutical products and medical devices in the United States, has approved a range of uses (indications) for dermal fillers. The most common indications include the treatment (reduction) of these common facial complaints:

  • Nasolabial folds, also known as smile lines (lines between the nose and corners of the mouth).
  • Marionette lines (lines at the sides of the mouth).
  • Chin lines.
  • Frown lines.
  • Loss of cheek volume (sunken or hollow cheeks).
  • Thin or asymmetric lips
  • Smoker’s lines (lines between the upper lip and nose).
  • Acne scars and certain other types of facial scars.
Injectable fillers may also be used to treat volume loss, wrinkles, and other cosmetic or age-related conditions of the hands.

Dermal Filler Types

Several different types of dermal fillers are approved for use by the FDA. Each has a discrete set of indications and none is indicated for the treatment of all the conditions described above.

In addition to its FDA-approved indications, each filler type has its own set of characteristics, including the nature and duration of results produced, that may make it more or less suitable for any given patient. Filler treatment candidates should consult with their providers prior to treatment to determine the appropriate filler and formulation for their needs.

Hyaluronic Acid (HA). Hyaluronic acid filler is the most widely used temporary dermal filler in North America. Due to its efficacy in the treatment of mild to moderate facial lines and wrinkles, it’s often referred to as a “wrinkle filler.” Its active ingredient is produced naturally by the body and dissipates relatively quickly, leaving skin temporarily plumper and better-hydrated. HA fillers’ effects last between 6 and 18 months, depending on the condition being treated, the treatment dose, and other factors specific to the patient. Lower-dose touch-ups may prolong results. Hyaluronic acid injection is also the most easily reversible of all filler treatment types; clinicians may inject an HA-dissolving enzyme (hyaluronidase) to turn back the visible effects of HA treatment.

Calcium Hydroxylapatite (CaHa). Calcium hydroxylapatite (CaHa) stimulates collagen production around the injection site to lessen the appearance of deeper lines, wrinkles, scars, and hollow areas. Like HA, CaHa occurs naturally in the body (mainly in the bones) and does not leave permanent traces of its activity in patients. Its collagen-stimulating effects tend to last longer than HA’s hydrating effects — 12 to 24 months, depending on the patient, condition, and dosing.

Poly L Lactic Acid. Poly-l-lactic acid also stimulates the production of collagen around the injection site. Unlike CaHa, it’s not naturally found in the body — poly-l-lactic acid is a widely used “biocompatible” synthetic that’s also found in dissolvable stitches. As a biocompatible, it rarely causes significant skin or tissue reactions in patients and is not considered a significant allergy risk (though allergic reactions have occurred after poly-l-lactic acid treatments). Because it produces a robust response, it’s favored for the treatment of deep wrinkles and scars, and its effects last longer than HA or CaHa fillers — two years or longer.

Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA). PMMA is another synthetic biocompatible that produces the longest-lasting effects of the four filler types described here. Indeed, because the “microspheres” containing PMMA are highly stable and do not dissipate after injection, PMMA filler is considered permanent. Bellafill®, the sole PMMA filler approved for use in the United States, also contains bovine collagen, which helps convey a more natural appearance of increased volume.

Although this is not an exhaustive list of dermal filler types available globally, it does cover the four main products commonly considered “dermal fillers” and approved for use in the United States by the FDA.

Certain other non-injectable medical products may be discussed in the same conversation as injectable dermal fillers, including the Permalip lip implant. Likewise, certain other minimally invasive facial augmentation treatments are (or were, in the case of collagen injections) considered as alternatives or complements to injectable dermal filler treatments.

Collagen Injections vs. Dermal Fillers

Other types of volume-enhancing injection procedures, such as collagen injections, are not widely used by board-certified clinicians in North America. Bellafill is the only FDA-approved filler product that actually contains collagen.

While patients and even some clinicians may casually describe filler brands such as Radiesse® as “collagen fillers,” this is not technically true. Radiesse merely stimulates the body’s natural collagen production rather than add foreign collagen. Due in part to allergy concerns and in part to the arrival of other types of wrinkle fillers on the market, once-popular collagen injections like Zyplast and Zyderm are no longer available in the United States.

Botox vs. Dermal Fillers

According to the American Society of Plastic Surgeons, injectable cosmetic treatments derived from botulinum toxin — principally Botox — are the most popular type of minimally invasive cosmetic injectable on the U.S. market. Board-certified clinicians completed more than 7 million botulinum toxin procedures in 2018, more than twice total number of dermal filler procedures.

Aside from the obvious pharmacological differences between these two treatment types, Botox and dermal fillers vary in both the mechanism and duration of the results they produce. Specifically, Botox results generally do not last as long between treatments — as little as three to six months, depending on dosing, treatment area, and other factors. HA fillers, the shortest-acting type of dermal filler, typically generate results for at least six months from treatment.

Botox and dermal fillers also work differently. Whereas dermal fillers temporarily hydrate the treated area (HA fillers), stimulate collagen production (CaHa and poly-l-lactic acid), or use biocompatible microstructures to add volume (poly-l-lactic acid and PMMA), Botox works by temporarily paralyzing facial muscles around the injection site, smoothing the skin as a result. Botox often produces more noticeable results for conditions involving finer lines, such as crow’s feet.

Autologous Filler Treatments vs. Pharmaceutical Dermal Fillers

Autologous facial fillers straddle the line between facial fillers and implants. However they are defined, autologous fillers use the patient’s own fat or skin — usually taken from the thigh or abdomen — to increase volume and/or smooth lines and wrinkles at the treatment site. Fat and skin fillers are applied via a grafting procedure that, while somewhat more invasive than injectable filler treatments, can generally be completed in an outpatient setting using local anesthetic only.

Due to the low risk of local or systemic reaction, the natural appearance of both fat and skin, and their effective permanence, clinicians and patients may prefer autologous fillers to pharmaceutical options.

Dermal Filler Results, Follow-up, and Reversal

Dermal filler treatments produce visible results within days to a few weeks of treatment, although the actual time to see desired results can vary considerably by filler type, condition, and patient expectations.

On the question of how long dermal fillers last: Dermal filler results generally last for a minimum of six months. Fillers other than hyaluronic acid produce results for longer periods of time: 12 to 24 months for CaHa fillers, two years or longer for poly-l-lactic acid fillers, and indefinitely for permanent facial fillers like PMMA and autologous implants.

Many dermal filler treatments require multiple in-office procedures to produce the expected results. These visits typically occur over a period of a few weeks to a few months and may be followed by later “touch-up” visits during which lower doses of filler are administered to maintain or sharpen results. To ensure the results and process remain in line with their expectations, clinicians should thoroughly apprise patients of the likelihood of multiple injection visits and touch-ups.

Even if follow-up or touch-up injections are not required by the patient’s treatment plan, clinicians should follow up with patients periodically to ensure they are satisfied with the results and identify possible side effects or complications (described in greater detail below).

Patients dissatisfied with the results of HA filler treatment or experiencing complications from HA filler treatment have reliable recourse: hyaluronidase injection, which can reverse the effects of hyaluronic acid. Reversing the effects of dermal fillers that stimulate collagen production or leave microspheres under the skin is not as straightforward. It is the clinician’s responsibility to communicate the potential risks and drawbacks of non-HA fillers and ensure that the patient fully understands these procedures’ implications.

Dermal Filler Brands, Formulations, and Indications

Dermal filler treatment candidates have no shortage of FDA-approved options. Although HA fillers are by far the most common type administered in the United States, each of the four main filler types is available through at least one manufacturer.

The top brands and formulations of fillers include:

Juvederm®

Manufactured by Allergan, Juvederm is a large brand family of hyaluronic acid fillers. Its formulations include:

  • Juvederm VOLUMA, indicated to increase volume in the cheeks
  • Juvederm VOLUMA XC, indicated to increase volume in the chin and cheeks
  • Juvederm XC, indicated for the treatment of moderate to severe lines around the mouth
  • Juvederm VOLBELLA XC, indicated for the treatment of perioral lines (lipstick lines) and temporary lip augmentation
  • Juvederm ULTRA XC, indicated for temporary lip augmentation (it is one of the most popular types of lip fillers available in the U.S.) and for the treatment of moderate to severe facial lines
  • Juvederm VOLLURE XC, indicated for the treatment of smile lines and parentheses lines around the sides of the mouth
Juvederm works like other HA fillers: by temporarily hydrating and plumping skin in the treated area without leaving permanent traces of its work.

Restylane®

Manufactured by Galderma, Restylane is Juvederm’s principal HA competitor. Like Juvederm, it offers several different formulations, including three types of lip injections and multiple injections for the treatment of lines and wrinkles elsewhere on the face.

Restylane formulations include:

  • Restylane (Original), indicated for the treatment of facial wrinkles and folds around the nose, mouth, and chin, and for subtle lip enhancement
  • Restylane Lyft, indicated to treat certain facial wrinkles and folds, to add volume to the cheeks, and to reduce wasting on the backs of the hands
  • Restylane Silk, indicated to reduce lining around the mouth and for subtle, natural-looking lip enhancement
  • Restylane Refyne, indicated to correct certain facial wrinkles and folds
  • Restylane Defyne, indicated to correct certain facial wrinkles and folds
  • Restylane Kysse, Restylane’s newest formulation — indicated for subtle lip enhancement

Belotero®

Belotero, marketed under the brand name Belotero Balance, is an HA filler manufactured by Merz Pharmaceuticals. Though less widely used than Restylane and Juvederm formulations, Belotero is indicated to treat moderate to severe nasolabial folds and is preferred by patients and providers who’ve had uneven results with its principal competitors.

Prevelle® (Prevelle Silk)

Prevelle Silk is another less widely used HA filler indicated to treat moderate to severe nasolabial folds and other facial line and wrinkle conditions. Prevelle Silk contains a lower concentration of hyaluronic acid than most Juvederm or Restylane formulations, so its results may fade more quickly than higher-dose alternatives.

Radiesse®

Radiesse is a CaHa filler manufactured by Merz Pharmaceuticals. Its two formulations, Radiesse and Radiesse (+), are indicated to correct moderate to severe nasolabial folds, to restore or increase volume in the lower face (chin and cheeks), and to increase volume (reduce lining and wasting) in the hands.

Sculptra®

Manufactured by Galderma, Sculptra is a poly-l-lactic acid filler indicated to treat shallow to severe nasolabial folds. It has been approved for use in Europe for more than 20 years, making it one of the longest-running dermal fillers still in use today. As a biocompatible synthetic filler, its results last significantly longer than HA fillers; a Galderma survey found that 80% of Sculptra patients remained satisfied with their results 25 months after treatment.

Bellafill® (Formerly ArteFill)

Bellafill is a permanent PMMA filler formerly known as ArteFill (prior to a 2014 rebrand). Manufactured by Suneva Medical, it is the only dermal filler specifically indicated for the treatment of acne scars. Bellafill is also indicated to treat nasolabial folds. As a synthetic PMMA filler, Bellafill is effectively permanent.

Refer to the American Board of Cosmetic Surgery’s list of widely used dermal filler brands and manufacturers for more information about these and other filler products.

Possible Injectable Filler Side Effects and Complications

Although dermal fillers are generally regarded as low-risk and do not require invasive surgery, they — like all medical treatments — do have some known side effects and complications.

The most common side effects of dermal fillers are mild and temporary, resolving without medical intervention in a matter of a few days to a few weeks. They include:

  • Itching or redness around the injection site
  • Minor to moderate bruising near the injection site
  • Minor bleeding from the injection site
  • Tenderness around the injection site
  • Mild swelling near the injection site
More serious dermal filler side effects or complications that may require medical attention include:
  • Skin or soft tissue infection associated with the injection site
  • Skin discoloration or “bluing” around the injection site
  • Necrosis (skin death) at the injection site, characterized by blackened skin
  • Prolonged bleeding from the injection site
  • Subdermal lumps or bumps (granules) around the injection site
  • Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis), characterized by skin swelling, rash, and/or difficulty breathing
  • Adverse blood clotting events, including stroke
  • Temporary or permanent vision changes or blindness
Certain dermal filler side effects and complications are associated with specific filler types. For example, skin granules are more likely to appear after PMMA treatment.

Additionally, certain serious filler complications are associated with improper injection process or technique. For example, inadequate pretreatment cleaning and sterilization may increase the risk of post-treatment infection. Adverse blood clotting events and vision changes are much more likely to occur when fillers are injected into a blood vessel, which the FDA and filler manufacturers specifically advise against.

Dermal Filler Contraindications: Who Should Avoid Dermal Fillers?

The list of dermal filler contraindications is not particularly long. However, prospective patients should defer or rethink treatment in specific situations, including but not limited to:

  • Age under 22 years
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Known allergy to any of the components of dermal filler injections, including lidocaine (many dermal filler formulations contain lidocaine, a local anesthetic)
  • Known propensity for excessive scarring (keloids or hypertrophic scars)
  • Current or recent skin infection or inflammatory allergic reaction
  • Known joint or tendon disease (if treatment is to occur near a joint or tendon)
  • Planned dental work in the near future (which can disturb recently injected filler material)
Prospective patients should consult their treatment provider to determine whether any of these contraindications (or any not listed) apply to them and the most advisable course of action to take should that be the case.

Some contraindications, such as pregnancy or a transient skin infection, are temporary rather than permanent. Others, such as an allergy to lidocaine, may permanently rule out treatment. Providers should take care to educate patients about the composition and allergen profile of the patient’s chosen filler, as some types of filler have potential allergens not found in others).
